# Editor Guide

As an Editor, you receive manuscripts that have passed the admin checklist and completed peer review. Your role is to read the reviewer reports, assess the manuscript, and send a decision to the author.

## 1. Your dashboard

Navigate to `/journal/[acronym]/editorial/editor`.

Your dashboard shows manuscripts awaiting your decision, grouped by status:
- **Awaiting decision** — all reviews received
- **Revision submitted** — author has resubmitted a revised manuscript
- **Pending escalation** — manuscripts you have flagged for EIC review

---

## 2. Reading reviewer reports

Click a manuscript to open its detail page. The review section shows:

- Each reviewer's recommendation (Accept / Minor Revision / Major Revision / Reject)
- Confidential comments to the editor
- Comments to the author
- Any attached files (annotated PDFs, etc.)

Reviewer identities are shown according to your journal's blinding policy.

---

## 3. Making a decision

Decisions available to editors:

| Decision | When to use |
|---|---|
| **Accept** | The manuscript is ready for publication. Reviewers are satisfied and no further revisions are required. |
| **Minor Revision** | Small, specific changes are required. You will check the revisions yourself — the manuscript is unlikely to go back to reviewers. |
| **Major Revision** | Substantial changes are required. The revised manuscript will be re-reviewed, normally by the same reviewers. |
| **Reject** | The manuscript is not suitable for the journal. Be specific in the decision letter about the reasons. |
| **Reject with Transfer** | The manuscript is not suitable here but may fit a partner journal. A transfer offer is sent automatically. |
| **Escalate to EIC** | The decision requires the Editor-in-Chief's involvement — e.g. a contested COI, an appeal, an unusual case. |

Your decision does not have to match the reviewer consensus. Reviewers advise; you decide.

## 5. Handling revised manuscripts

When an author resubmits a revision, you receive a notification and the manuscript reappears in your queue with:
- The revised manuscript files
- The author's response letter addressing each reviewer comment
- The original reviewer reports for comparison

For **minor revisions**, you can typically assess the changes yourself and make a final decision without re-reviewing.

For **major revisions**, the revised manuscript is normally sent back to the original reviewers. The system handles the reviewer re-invitation automatically — you confirm the re-review and the workflow continues.

---

## 6. Escalating to the Editor-in-Chief

Use the **Escalate to EIC** decision when the case requires editorial oversight beyond your authority:

- A formal appeal from an author disputing a rejection
- A post-submission conflict of interest disclosure
- Suspected research misconduct
- An unusual split in reviewer recommendations requiring senior judgment
- Any case where you are uncertain and want a second opinion

Add a note explaining why you are escalating. The EIC receives a notification and the manuscript appears in their queue.

---

## 7. When reviewers disagree

Strong disagreement between reviewers is normal in academic publishing. Some guidance:

- Read the arguments carefully, not just the recommendations
- A reviewer who explains their concerns in detail carries more weight than one who asserts a conclusion
- You may invite a third reviewer to break a genuine impasse — discuss with the EIC if unsure
- Do not average the recommendations mechanically; make a judgment
